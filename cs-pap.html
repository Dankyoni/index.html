Situation

Reassessing a China PAP Business at a Category Inflection Point

Background
A Japanese medical device manufacturer with existing China operations needed to reassess its PAP (Positive Airway Pressure / sleep therapy device) business strategy. The category was intensifying: domestic brands had scaled aggressively via EC-first strategies, the global premium leader was deepening hospital and distributor relationships, and the client's own sales trajectory and return rates signalled an urgent need for repositioning.
The Work
Full two-chapter analysis under the NRI consulting framework — (1) China PAP market data analysis covering overall sizing, online channel structure (Tmall), brand GMV concentration, and price band dynamics; and (2) a three-brand leading company study examining commercial models, channel architecture, P&L structure, KPI design, distribution, promotion, service, and org structure — with client internal gaps mapped against benchmarks.
Deliverable
~45-slide executive steering pack delivered to client leadership to reassess mid-term product strategy, channel investment priorities, and organizational structure for the China PAP business.

Chapter 01 — Market Data Analysis

Online Channel Structure & Price Band Concentration

The China home PAP market has grown at a sustained pace over the observed period. Online channels — led by Tmall — account for a significant and rising proportion of total category sales. Top-5 brand concentration on Tmall is high, with the gap between leaders and smaller players widening over time. APAP and BiPAP serve structurally different patient profiles: APAP skews online and female-purchase (family buying for a partner), while BiPAP is more physician-referred and purchased by the patient themselves.

Chapter 02 — Leading Company Study

Three Competitive Archetypes — How Market Leaders Win

Three brands were selected to represent the dominant competitive archetypes in the China home PAP market. Each archetype reflects a distinct commercial logic — the analysis went beyond surface metrics to examine how channel decisions, KPI design, distributor management, and service architecture combine to create sustainable competitive positions.

Archetype A
Premium Foreign Specialist
High-end brand equity built via hospital physician relationships
EC used primarily for traffic acquisition on entry-level APAP; high-margin BiPAP protected exclusively offline
Separate EC legal entity with dedicated pre-sales consultation (8–10 specialists) — brand education before purchase is the core conversion lever
Distributor profit protection as strategic priority: online channel kept minimal to protect offline margins; distributor KPIs include service capability and inventory management
Post-purchase: 7-day intensive follow-up to overcome return window — "adherence" as the core retention metric
Archetype B
Domestic EC-Native Challenger
EC-first build: heavy early-stage traffic investment to acquire market share rapidly
Full price-range lineup across APAP and BiPAP, supported by respiratory therapy as a dedicated company business unit
KPI priority: GMV and growth rate above margin — Sell-In to distributors as primary accountability metric
Consequence: price management historically weak — Sell-In pressure leads to inventory overload, triggering grey-market outflows
Hospital channel attempted post-COVID but exited as product spec gaps became evident against clinical requirements
Archetype C
Value Offline Specialist
Low-price positioning enabled by OEM cost structure — strong production cost management
Offline-first distribution: exclusive distributor model with Sell-Out KPIs built into distributor agreements — tracks inventory turnover, not just Sell-In
Distributor profit protection: specific SKUs assigned exclusively to offline channel, preventing online undercutting
Hospital channel built systematically via physician education and dedicated service engineering at distributor level
Channel management discipline as the core competency — not brand or product
The leading company study revealed that brand equity and channel profitability are structurally linked in this category. Brands that sacrifice channel discipline to chase volume end up with price erosion that undermines both hospital physician trust and offline distributor commitment — a compounding disadvantage.
Chapter 02 continued — Client Implications & Internal Gap Analysis

Diagnosing the Gaps Against the Competitive Benchmark

Mapped against the three-archetype benchmark, the client's China PAP business showed structural gaps across organizational commitment, EC operating model, and product competitiveness. These were documented through internal interviews with EC planning, store operations, and after-sales leadership.

Organizational Commitment
PAP below the analysis threshold
PAP not treated as a priority category — volume below the threshold for regular management review. No dedicated PAP headcount across EC planning, after-sales, or product management. Analysis and decision-making defaulted to larger categories.
EC Operating Model
Conversion rate and return rate gaps
Store CVR significantly below category benchmark. Return rate above 30% — driven by inadequate pre-purchase consultation and post-purchase setup support. Purchase inquiry content not systematically analyzed; no PAP-dedicated pre-sales team.
Product Competitiveness
Spec and SKU range gaps
Limited SKU range relative to leading competitors. Key functional gaps: data transfer capability, mobile app connectivity, humidity management. Warranty period shorter than market standard. No trial/return policy to reduce purchase risk.
Promotional Investment
Traffic investment below effective threshold
Monthly EC traffic spend far below the estimated per-unit acquisition cost typical for this category. Large-scale promotional events not feasible at current scale — creating a cycle where low visibility suppresses the volume needed to justify investment.
The steering pack concluded: addressing organizational commitment and pre/post-sales service capability were higher-priority levers than price reduction. Price cuts without specialist support would not close the conversion gap — the purchase decision in this category is driven by clinical trust and usage confidence, not price alone.
