As Values Rebuild: Can a Brand Become Someone's "Nearby"?

June 2026  ·  Written in Bochum, Germany

Lying flat is not laziness — it's silent resistance.

For thirty years, the logic held: score well on the gaokao, get into a good university, find a good job, and life gets better. Young people like me who left villages to study and work in big cities — our parents might have worked in factories or farmed the land, but as long as the child got that diploma, the next generation's trajectory could be different. This was more than a cultural expectation; it was a social contract, and two generations organized their entire lives around it.

Young people are waking up. They no longer let involution — studying longer, working more overtime, giving more — define their lives, because the promised "outcome" keeps moving further away. Even now, putting in ten times the effort of our parents' generation, we get back a fraction of what they received. The mainstream value system that defined "success" as the only possible answer has quietly started to lose its grip. More and more people simply want to live well and be happy — and have decided that is enough.

So lying flat is not just an individual choice to opt out. It is a collective, silent protest against a system that has stopped delivering. At its core, what young people are doing is: taking the values they were taught, examining each one, and rebuilding from what actually holds up. Rebuilding life, starting from the smallest unit — the individual. Finding their way back to a nearby that had disappeared.

Xiang Biao, Director of the Max Planck Institute for Social Anthropology in Germany, describes another dimension of this rupture: modern people have lost the near world — the textured social reality of recognisable neighbours, the breakfast stand on the corner, relationships genuinely connected to daily life. Our eyes jump from the screen to distant success stories, distant trends, distant narratives — and the nearby world in between has shrunk.

This loss looks different in different cities.

In Tokyo, the nearby is remarkably easy to build. Not because Japanese people are particularly sociable, but because the city's physical structure has preserved vast numbers of individually-owned small spaces: the tofu shop is just the tofu shop, the vegetable shop is just the vegetable shop, the neighbourhood sento (public bathhouse) always has its regulars. You go to buy vegetables, exchange a few words with the vendor; you go for a bath, exchange a nod with the stranger beside you. These connections — too faint to even call relationships — form a kind of background to life: a low-density sense of belonging. Even without living in a centre like Shibuya or Roppongi, just stepping outside, you can naturally build your own nearby.

Germany is completely different. After moving here, I found that most places to buy food are chain supermarkets — nothing like Japan's small specialist shops or China's wet markets. The cashier faces hundreds of different people each day; they won't know you, and you won't know them. No regular vegetable vendor, no small restaurant where they know your order before you say it. The spaces are large and well-stocked, but the human texture that makes life feel inhabited is absent.

China's situation is more specific. The individual small shops existed — but e-commerce's explosive growth nearly destroyed them. When everything can be ordered on a phone and arrive tomorrow, the pull of going out weakens, the density of offline space drops, and those everyday occasions for accidentally building a nearby — buying groceries, getting an early breakfast, a street-side haircut — quietly disappear. E-commerce gave people efficiency. The cost was that life became more convenient and more isolated at the same time.

This is the double loss facing young people in China: the success narrative has lost its spell, and the near world of daily life has already shrunk. These two things together mean that "buying things" has started losing the meaning it once carried — not just because young people have less money, but because the logic of building identity and moving upward through consumption has imploded for many of them.

This isn't frugality. It's a reordering of values: I am willing to pay for things that genuinely matter.

The rise of UNIQLO, the Japanese basic-wear brand, is often read as a value-for-money story. That's not quite right. More accurately: as the philosophical foundation of conspicuous consumption began to crack, UNIQLO offered not cheapness but "quality that needs no explanation." You wear UNIQLO not because you can't afford anything else, but because you no longer need to prove anything through your clothes. This is a philosophical shift in consumer behaviour — its relationship with price is less direct than it looks.

The rise of space-based consumption follows the same logic. niko and..., the Japanese lifestyle concept store, doesn't sell clothes — it sells an afternoon to spend inside. The independent bookrooms appearing across Chengdu and Hangzhou don't sell books — they sell a kind of relationship that can happen there: with the space, with the stranger who sits across from you, with yourself on a particular afternoon. All of it is "looking for a nearby" — people actively rebuilding the near, anchored world.

The logic behind IP collaborations and lifestyle brands is the same. People aren't buying product functions — they're buying confirmation that "I am someone like this." But as collaborations flood the market to the point of numbness, what survives are the ones that genuinely represent a value position, not those just chasing traffic. Consumers are using sharper judgement to protect their limited budget of belonging.

The story of WINONA (a Chinese clinically-focused skincare brand) and PROYA (a Chinese mass-market skincare brand) is essentially this: they didn't win on price — they won on resonance. In their consumer communication, they raise social questions and life questions — this isn't just product positioning, it's saying: our values are aligned. When that conversation is real rather than a marketing wrapper, young consumers can tell the difference.

If values are being rebuilt and consumption is being reordered, the most important thing a brand can do is no longer make you feel that owning it moves you up a tier. It's to become a trustworthy endorsement of a way of living.

That is much harder than it sounds. A way of living can't be designed — it can only be lived. Whether a brand finds a genuine place in a consumer's life depends not on how well the slogan is written, but on whether it truly knows what it stands for — and holds that consistency in every detail.

This brings me back to Xiang Biao's "nearby."

Not the distant goal she's been striving toward. Not the identity signal she wants to perform for the world. But something closer — something that exists in the actual texture of her daily life, needs no explanation, where trust grows on its own.

Can a brand become someone's nearby? Can it become the thing that needs no introduction, that appears naturally in the fabric of daily life, that makes someone feel a real connection?

I think it can. But the condition is: you first have to know who you are, what you stand for, what kind of value world your consumers are rebuilding — and what genuine role you can play in it.
