Situation

Why This Review Was Needed

A brand competing in China's electric toothbrush market faced a strategic inflection point: the category was shifting online rapidly, price bands were consolidating on Tmall, and EC-native domestic competitors were scaling share aggressively. Leadership needed hard evidence — not anecdotal sales narratives — to reset the mid-term plan.

The brief required answers to four questions: Where is the market going? How is the online channel structured? What are competitors doing commercially? And where are the internal gaps in organizational structure, EC operations, and after-sales that are holding the brand back?

Analytical note: This public case study applies the same two-chapter consulting framework — market data analysis followed by leading-company study and client implications — that I used in executive steering packs at NRI Shanghai, here applied to publicly available data.
Chapter 1 — Market Data Analysis

Market Sizing & Online Channel Structure

China's electric toothbrush market has grown from ~RMB 4.5bn (2019) to an estimated ~RMB 9–10bn by 2024, driven by oral-care awareness, premiumisation, and EC penetration. Online channels — led by Tmall — now account for ~55–60% of category sales, with TikTok commerce gaining meaningful share since 2022.

Chapter 2 continued — Internal Gap Analysis

Organizational Structure, EC Operating Model & After-Sales Gaps

Beyond the external competitive picture, the steering pack examined where internal structure was limiting the brand's ability to compete effectively online. Three gap areas were identified:

Organizational Structure
No dedicated EC team structure
EC and offline planning sit within the same team, creating conflicting KPIs and slow response to platform dynamics. A dedicated EC pod — with its own P&L visibility — is needed to compete with EC-native brands operating at pace.
EC Ops
Traffic allocation skewed to standard feed
~80% of Tmall ad spend goes to standard search feed, with minimal investment in SNS/social traffic (TikTok, Redbook). EC-native competitors run the inverse — content-led traffic at lower CPM, with paid search as a conversion layer, not the primary acquisition driver.
After-Sales
AS capacity not scaled for EC volume
After-sales infrastructure was designed for offline retail volumes. As EC share grows, return rates and service enquiries are increasing faster than capacity. Without dedicated EC after-sales headcount and clear SLAs, this becomes a review and retention liability.
KPIs
Cross-functional KPI visibility is limited
Sales, marketing, and operations teams track different metrics with no shared dashboard. This makes it difficult for leadership to identify where the EC funnel is leaking — awareness, conversion, or post-purchase — and to allocate resources accordingly.
Improvement Priorities — Action List

Recommendations Across Organizational Structure, EC & After-Sales

The steering pack closed with a prioritised action list across three workstreams, sequenced by impact and implementation complexity:

Workstream 1 — Brand Positioning vs. Value Competitors
1
Develop efficacy-led content that clearly differentiates from value-tier competitors — not on price, but on clinical trust, build quality, and after-sales confidence
2
Seed mid-tier KOLs (100k–500k followers) with product-in-use content; shift from brand-awareness KOLs to conversion-focused creators in the RMB 300–500 price band
3
Clarify price-band architecture — reduce SKU overlap between tiers to eliminate internal channel conflict and simplify the consumer decision
Workstream 2 — Tmall EC Operating Model
1
Rebalance traffic allocation: shift from ~80% standard feed to a mix of ~50% standard feed / ~30% SNS (TikTok, Redbook) / ~20% live-commerce, tested over one campaign cycle
2
Build a shared EC KPI dashboard across sales, marketing, and ops — tracking funnel metrics from impressions to post-purchase reviews in one view
3
Stand up a dedicated EC pod with its own P&L — separate from offline planning — to enable faster test-and-learn cycles on pricing, content, and promotional mechanics
Workstream 3 — After-Sales Capacity & Communication
1
Add dedicated EC after-sales headcount scaled to online volume — with clear SLAs for response time, return processing, and repair turnaround
2
Move after-sales policy (warranty, repair, return) to front-of-page on Tmall flagship — treat it as a conversion asset, not a compliance footnote
3
Pilot a post-purchase adherence programme (usage tips, check-in messaging, accessory reminders) to improve retention and reduce churn-driven negative reviews
Steering outcome: These three workstreams form a sequenced mid-term plan — brand repositioning first (6 months), EC operating model rebuild second (6–12 months), after-sales scaling third (12–18 months) — giving leadership a clear investment roadmap with measurable milestones at each stage.
