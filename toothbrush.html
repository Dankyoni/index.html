Situation

Understanding Patient Access to Make a Market Entry Decision

Background
A Japanese medical device manufacturer evaluating market expansion needed to understand the Patient Assistance Program (PAP) landscape before committing to an entry strategy. The core question: what access barriers exist for target patients, how do leading manufacturers address them, and what program design would best fit this client's product and commercial model?
The Work
Built a full PAP landscape analysis: patient access barrier mapping, competitive PAP benchmarking across leading medical device players, program design options and trade-offs, and implementation pathway — delivered as an executive steering pack for C-level decision-making.
Deliverable
~45-slide steering pack covering access landscape, competitive programs, recommended PAP structure, and phased rollout plan. Used by client leadership to make a go/no-go market entry decision.
Approach — 4-Phase Analysis

From Access Barrier Mapping to Program Design

Phase 01
Patient Access Barrier Mapping
Identified and categorized structural barriers to patient access: financial, reimbursement coverage, clinical pathway, and geographic reach
Phase 02
Competitive PAP Benchmarking
Analyzed existing PAP structures from leading medical device manufacturers — eligibility criteria, benefit scope, enrollment mechanics, and funding models
Phase 03
Program Design Options
Developed 3 PAP design scenarios across cost, coverage scope, and administrative complexity — with pros/cons and fit assessment against client's commercial constraints
Phase 04
Implementation Pathway
Defined phased rollout plan, stakeholder engagement sequence (HCP, payer, patient advocacy), and KPIs to track program effectiveness post-launch
PAP design decisions are not just about patient benefit — they are commercial strategy decisions. The right program structure directly affects reimbursement negotiation leverage, physician adoption, and long-term market share trajectory.
Access Barrier Landscape

Patient Access Barrier Categories

The analysis structured patient access barriers across four categories. Each drives a different PAP design response — a program addressing only financial barriers while ignoring clinical pathway barriers will underperform in markets where physician access is the primary bottleneck.

Relative weight of each barrier category varies significantly by market and patient segment — the steering pack mapped this variance to match PAP design to where the access gap is largest.
